A VISUAL GUIDE TO PENNY STOCKS

Understanding the definition, risks, and landscape of stocks trading under $5 in the USA.

The Core Definition: The < $5 Rule

In the United States, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) defines a penny stock with a simple but critical benchmark:

< $5.00

PER SHARE

This price tag is a regulatory tripwire that triggers specific investor protection rules due to the high risks involved.

Price vs. Value: A Common Misconception

A low share price does not automatically mean a small company. **Market Capitalization** (Price × Shares) reveals the company's true size.

Scenario A: "Penny Stock" by Price

A Large Company

$4.00 / share

x 1 Billion Shares

$4 Billion

Scenario B: Not a Penny Stock

A Smaller Company

$200 Million

Where They Trade: The Market Landscape

Most penny stocks are not on major exchanges like the NYSE or Nasdaq. They primarily trade on Over-the-Counter (OTC) markets, which have varying levels of transparency.

High-Risk Profile

Penny stocks are defined by characteristics that make them exceptionally risky compared to established, large-cap stocks.

Fraud Alert: The "Pump & Dump" Scheme

This common fraud exploits low liquidity and lack of information to manipulate prices and deceive investors.

1. Accumulation: Fraudsters quietly buy large amounts of a stock at a very low price.
2. The Pump: They spread false hype via social media and email to drive up the price.
3. The Dump: As new investors buy in, the fraudsters sell all their shares at the inflated peak price.
4. The Crash: The price plummets, leaving everyone else with worthless stock.

Investor Safety Checklist

🔎

Research Deeply

Verify company info independently. Use the SEC's EDGAR database.

🚫

Reject Pressure

Ignore "guaranteed return" promises and high-pressure sales tactics.

💧

Check Liquidity

Avoid thinly traded stocks. If volume is low, you may not be able to sell.

💸

Invest Only What You Can Lose

Assume you could lose your entire investment. This is speculation, not saving.
